# Dispatches from the Record: Clerk Notes No. 15

## Presidential Immunity

### Not your Father’s Doctrine

## What is this?

This is a clerk-note style public doctrine translation about presidential immunity.

It does not try to restate the doctrine technically from top to bottom. It asks a structural question first:

**Immunity for what function?**

The note argues that presidential immunity is coherent only if it remains attached to lawful office-function. It may protect the presidency’s ability to act. It may not convert the office into permanent non-answerability for the human officeholder.

## Why should I care?

Presidential immunity is often argued through slogans.

One side says: no one is above the law.

Another side says: the presidency cannot function if every hard executive decision becomes personal legal exposure.

Both instincts point at something real. Neither is precise enough by itself.

This note gives the reader a cleaner distinction:

**The office may need protection. The person occupying the office cannot become unanswerable as a person.**

## What is the claim?

The core claim is:

**Presidential immunity may protect lawful office-function. It may not convert office into non-answerability for the human officeholder.**

The central test is:

**What public executive function did the act carry?**

Official form may trigger the question. It cannot answer the question.

## What is this not?

This note is not legal advice.

It is not a complete technical restatement of presidential-immunity doctrine.

It does not claim that every form of presidential immunity is incoherent.

It does not deny that the presidency may require protection from legal process that would disable lawful executive function.

It does not collapse civil damages immunity, criminal immunity, evidentiary restriction, timing, official-act classification, core constitutional authority, and unofficial conduct into one rule.

It does not claim that motive alone decides whether an act belongs to the office.

It does not claim that political benefit automatically defeats office-function.

It does not claim that every hard mixed-capacity case is easy.

The note is narrower: it asks what presidential immunity must be, structurally, if it is going to remain coherent.

## How was this done?

The note separates the human officeholder from the constitutional office.

It then asks what function an act carried into the civic body:

* Did it carry law execution?
* Did it carry public administration?
* Did it carry constitutional command?
* Did it carry national representation?
* Did it carry emergency response?
* Did it carry a recognizable executive function that belongs to the office?

Or did it use official tools to carry something else, such as private gain, candidate benefit, retaliation, intimidation, concealment, factional advantage, or personal power?

The note then sorts failure through five Structural Flow failure regimes:

* Dissolution: the boundary between office and person collapses.
* Shear: the act is routed under the wrong category.
* Drift: protection migrates from function to status.
* Echo: the vocabulary of accountability remains while answerability disappears.
* Lock: public consequence travels outward, but accountability cannot travel back.

The key failure sequence is:

**Shear enables Lock. Lock makes Shear durable.**
